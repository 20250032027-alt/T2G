Worth knowing The glycemic index (GI) measures how quickly food raises blood sugar. Doctors generally advise diabetics to avoid foods with a GI of 55 and above. T2G Coco Sugar has a tested GI of 35, which puts it in the low range and makes it safe for most diabetics to use in moderation. Coconut Sugar and Health

GI 35. That is a tested figure, not a claim based on general coconut sugar data. Low GI means blood sugar rises more slowly than with refined sugar, which matters for diabetics and anyone managing their diet.
Generally yes, in moderation. Its GI of 35 is well below the 55 threshold that most doctors flag for diabetics. That said, it is still sugar, and individual responses vary. Talk to your doctor if you have specific health concerns.
Coconut sugar comes from coconut sap and retains minerals, vitamins, and amino acids through minimal processing. Muscovado comes from sugar cane and is more refined, which means it has less nutritional value. Coconut sugar also has a lower glycemic index than muscovado.
Both are natural sweeteners, but agave syrup is refined from the agave cactus and has a much higher fructose content than coco syrup. High fructose intake is harder on the liver and metabolic system over time. Coco syrup is the cleaner option for most people.

Virgin Coconut Oil

Virgin coconut oil is pressed from fresh coconut meat with no refining, bleaching, or deodorizing. The flavor, aroma, and nutrients stay intact. Regular coconut oil is processed using solvents and heat to maximize extraction, which strips out most of what makes coconut oil interesting in the first place.
